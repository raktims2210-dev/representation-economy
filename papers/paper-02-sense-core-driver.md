## A Canonical Architecture for Governable AI Systems

## Abstract

Most discussions on artificial intelligence focus on intelligence, reasoning, models, and automation. But enterprise AI does not fail only because models are weak. It often fails because reality is poorly represented, authority is poorly delegated, and execution is poorly governed.

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework explains the complete institutional architecture required for reliable AI systems.

- **SENSE** makes reality machine-legible.
- **CORE** reasons over that representation.
- **DRIVER** turns decisions into governed action.

This paper introduces SENSE–CORE–DRIVER as a canonical framework for understanding how AI systems move from real-world signals to institutional outcomes.

---

## 1. The Core Problem

AI systems are often treated as if intelligence alone creates value.

This is incomplete.

A model may reason well, but if it receives poor signals, incomplete context, weak identity, unclear authority, or unverified state, the outcome can still fail.

Enterprise AI requires more than prediction.

It requires:

- representation,
- reasoning,
- delegation,
- verification,
- execution,
- auditability,
- and recourse.

That is why AI architecture must be understood as a full institutional system, not merely as a model layer.

---

## 2. The Framework

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework has three layers.

```text
REALITY
Humans • Systems • Events • Assets • Documents • Sensors
        ↓
SENSE
Signal → ENtity → State → Evolution
        ↓
CORE
Comprehend → Optimize → Realize → Evolve
        ↓
DRIVER
Delegation → Representation → Identity → Verification → Execution → Recourse
        ↓
INSTITUTIONAL OUTCOME
3. SENSE: Making Reality Machine-Legible

SENSE is the legibility layer.

It converts messy reality into structured representation that machines can reason over.

SENSE includes:

Signal

The system detects events, changes, inputs, records, behaviors, documents, transactions, alerts, or observations.

Examples:

a payment delay,
a customer complaint,
a machine vibration,
a login anomaly,
a shipment status change,
a contract clause,
a support ticket.
ENtity

The signal is attached to a persistent entity.

Examples:

customer,
employee,
machine,
account,
invoice,
vendor,
document,
transaction,
software service.

Without entity resolution, signals remain isolated noise.

State Representation

The system builds a structured model of the current condition of the entity.

Examples:

account risk level,
customer eligibility,
asset health,
order status,
compliance exposure,
ticket priority,
operational bottleneck.
Evolution

The system updates this representation over time as new signals arrive.

Reality is not static.

A good AI system must understand how entities change.

4. CORE: Reasoning Over Representation

CORE is the cognition layer.

It is where AI models, rules, agents, workflows, and reasoning systems interpret the structured representation created by SENSE.

CORE includes:

Comprehend

The system understands context.

It interprets the state of an entity, the situation, and the relevant constraints.

Optimize

The system evaluates possible actions, trade-offs, priorities, and consequences.

Realize

The system forms an intent, recommendation, plan, or decision.

Evolve

The system learns from feedback, outcomes, exceptions, and new patterns.

CORE is powerful, but CORE is not enough.

Even strong reasoning can fail if the system lacks proper representation and governed execution.

5. DRIVER: Turning Intelligence into Governed Action

DRIVER is the legitimacy layer.

It determines whether an AI-generated recommendation or action should actually be executed.

DRIVER includes:

Delegation

Who authorized the system to act?

Was the authority given by a human, policy, process, institution, or contract?

Representation

What model of reality did the system use?

Was the representation complete, current, and valid?

Identity

Which entity is affected by the decision?

Is the entity correctly identified?

Verification

How is the decision checked before execution?

Is there validation, audit, human review, or automated control?

Execution

How is the action carried out?

Does it happen through an API, workflow, notification, approval, system update, or human action?

Recourse

What happens if the system is wrong?

Can the action be reversed, challenged, corrected, escalated, or compensated?

6. Why CORE Alone Is Dangerous

Many AI programs overinvest in CORE.

They focus on:

better models,
stronger reasoning,
larger context windows,
more agents,
more automation,
more prompts.

But enterprise failure often happens outside CORE.

A system can have a powerful model and still fail because:

SENSE is incomplete,
entities are wrongly represented,
state is outdated,
authority is unclear,
verification is weak,
execution is uncontrolled,
recourse is missing.

This is why governable AI requires SENSE, CORE, and DRIVER together.

7. Simple Example: Loan Approval

Consider an AI system assisting with loan approval.

SENSE

The system collects signals:

income records,
repayment history,
employment data,
existing liabilities,
transaction behavior,
document submissions.

It attaches these signals to the correct applicant and builds a current state representation.

CORE

The system reasons:

Is the applicant eligible?
What is the risk?
What amount may be suitable?
What exceptions need review?
DRIVER

The system governs action:

Who approved the AI’s use?
What policy applies?
Was the applicant correctly identified?
Was the decision verified?
Can the applicant appeal?
Is there an audit trail?

Without DRIVER, the AI may produce a decision but not a legitimate institutional action.

8. Simple Example: Enterprise IT Operations

An AI agent detects an application outage.

SENSE

It collects signals from logs, metrics, alerts, traces, and incident tickets.

It attaches those signals to the correct service, application, infrastructure node, and business process.

CORE

It reasons about root cause, impact, and possible remediation.

DRIVER

It decides:

whether the agent is authorized to restart a service,
whether approval is required,
whether the action affects customers,
whether rollback is available,
whether escalation is needed,
whether the action should be logged.

This is the difference between automation and governable autonomy.

9. Institutional AI Needs All Three Layers

AI value does not come only from prediction.

It comes from the ability to represent reality, reason over it, and act legitimately.

Layer	Core Question
SENSE	Is reality represented correctly?
CORE	Is the reasoning useful?
DRIVER	Is the action legitimate and governed?

A mature AI system must answer all three.

10. The Strategic Implication

The next generation of AI advantage will not belong only to organizations with the best models.

It will belong to organizations that build the best representation and governance architecture around intelligence.

That means:

better machine-legible reality,
better institutional context,
better decision boundaries,
better delegation mechanisms,
better verification,
better recourse,
better auditability.

This is the architectural foundation of the Representation Economy.

11. Conclusion

SENSE–CORE–DRIVER reframes AI from a model-centric technology into an institutional architecture.

The framework shows that enterprise AI must be designed across three layers:

SENSE: reality becomes machine-legible.
CORE: intelligence reasons over that representation.
DRIVER: governed action becomes institutionally legitimate.

Without SENSE, AI lacks reality.

Without CORE, AI lacks reasoning.

Without DRIVER, AI lacks legitimacy.

The future of enterprise AI will be shaped by organizations that understand all three.
