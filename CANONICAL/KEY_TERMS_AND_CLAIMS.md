# Key Terms and Canonical Claims

## Representation Economy & SENSE–CORE–DRIVER

This document defines the core terms, canonical statements, and foundational claims associated with the:

- Representation Economy
- SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework
- Institutional AI
- Machine-legible reality
- Enterprise AI legitimacy systems

These definitions are intended to create conceptual consistency across articles, presentations, research, discussions, derivative works, and future publications.

---

# Part I — Canonical Claims

---

# Canonical Claim 1

> AI systems do not act directly on reality.  
> They act on representations of reality.

This is the foundational claim of the Representation Economy.

AI systems operate on:

- data,
- embeddings,
- graphs,
- logs,
- state models,
- prompts,
- records,
- metadata,
- and contextual abstractions.

All AI reasoning is constrained by representation quality.

---

# Canonical Claim 2

> Better CORE cannot fully fix broken SENSE.

Smarter reasoning systems cannot completely compensate for:

- missing signals,
- weak entity resolution,
- stale state,
- fragmented context,
- incorrect mappings,
- or poor representation integrity.

Representation quality determines the ceiling of AI reliability.

---

# Canonical Claim 3

> The next era of AI competition may be decided less by raw intelligence alone and more by trusted representation systems.

As AI systems scale institutionally, competitive advantage increasingly depends on:

- representation quality,
- context infrastructure,
- governance,
- provenance,
- verification,
- legitimacy,
- and execution trust.

---

# Canonical Claim 4

> Governance is no longer external documentation alone.  
> Governance increasingly becomes part of runtime execution.

In autonomous and AI-mediated systems, governance must operate through:

- policy-aware orchestration,
- verification layers,
- identity-bound execution,
- runtime guardrails,
- auditability,
- rollback systems,
- and recourse mechanisms.

---

# Canonical Claim 5

> Intelligence does not automatically create legitimacy.

An AI system may be:

- accurate,
- intelligent,
- fast,
- and operationally useful,

yet still remain institutionally unacceptable if it lacks:

- authorization,
- verification,
- accountability,
- traceability,
- explainability,
- or recourse.

---

# Canonical Claim 6

> The future of AI may increasingly depend on representation integrity.

Representation integrity includes:

- correctness,
- provenance,
- consistency,
- contextual continuity,
- identity resolution,
- state freshness,
- and governance alignment.

Representation integrity may become as important as cybersecurity.

---

# Canonical Claim 7

> AI systems increasingly compete through representation infrastructure.

Examples include:

- entity graphs,
- semantic layers,
- context systems,
- digital twins,
- governance metadata,
- institutional memory,
- identity systems,
- and execution frameworks.

Representation infrastructure becomes a strategic layer.

---

# Canonical Claim 8

> SENSE determines what the system knows.  
> CORE determines how the system reasons.  
> DRIVER determines whether the system is allowed to act.

This is the simplest operational interpretation of the framework.

---

# Canonical Claim 9

> DRIVER is the legitimacy layer of AI systems.

DRIVER governs:

- delegation,
- identity,
- verification,
- execution,
- accountability,
- and recourse.

Without DRIVER, AI systems may remain operationally intelligent but institutionally unsafe.

---

# Canonical Claim 10

> The Representation Economy is not only about AI.  
> It is about machine-mediated institutional reality.

The Representation Economy studies how:

- institutions,
- enterprises,
- governments,
- markets,
- platforms,
- and infrastructures

become machine-legible and machine-operable.

# Part II — Canonical Definitions

---

# Representation Economy

## Canonical Definition

> The Representation Economy is an economic and institutional condition in which value, power, trust, governance, and competitive advantage increasingly depend on the creation, control, verification, updating, and operational use of machine-legible representations of reality.

---

# Machine-Legible Reality

## Definition

Machine-legible reality refers to reality after it has been transformed into representations that machines can process, interpret, reason over, and act upon.

Examples include:

- structured data,
- embeddings,
- graphs,
- state models,
- digital twins,
- enterprise records,
- semantic systems,
- and contextual abstractions.

---

# Representation Integrity

## Definition

Representation Integrity refers to the correctness, consistency, provenance, continuity, freshness, traceability, and governance alignment of machine representations.

Representation integrity determines whether an AI system is operating on trustworthy representations.

---

# Representation Fragmentation

## Definition

Representation Fragmentation occurs when multiple systems maintain inconsistent, incomplete, conflicting, or disconnected representations of the same entity, process, or institutional reality.

Fragmentation creates:

- operational inconsistency,
- reasoning failure,
- governance gaps,
- and trust breakdowns.

---

# Representation Saturation

## Definition

Representation Saturation refers to a condition where institutions produce so many representations, signals, and contextual abstractions that governance, verification, and human comprehension become difficult.

More data does not always create more clarity.

---

# Representation Monopolies

## Definition

Representation Monopolies emerge when organizations control critical machine-legible representations required for economic participation, coordination, identity, trust, or operational access.

Examples may include:

- search indices,
- identity systems,
- payment identity layers,
- platform reputation systems,
- enterprise graphs,
- or institutional registries.

---

# Representation Infrastructure

## Definition

Representation Infrastructure refers to the systems that create, maintain, synchronize, govern, verify, and operationalize machine-legible representations.

Examples include:

- identity systems,
- semantic layers,
- entity graphs,
- telemetry systems,
- governance metadata,
- state synchronization layers,
- digital twins,
- and institutional memory systems.

---

# Institutional Intelligence

## Definition

Institutional Intelligence refers to the ability of institutions to:

- sense reality,
- maintain representation continuity,
- reason over institutional context,
- govern execution,
- and evolve decisions responsibly over time.

Institutional intelligence is broader than model intelligence.

---

# Delegated Autonomy

## Definition

Delegated Autonomy refers to bounded authority granted to AI systems to make decisions or execute actions within defined institutional constraints.

Delegated autonomy requires:

- delegation,
- verification,
- identity control,
- governance,
- auditability,
- and recourse.

---

# AI Legitimacy Systems

## Definition

AI Legitimacy Systems are the governance structures that determine whether AI-generated actions are institutionally acceptable, accountable, reversible, traceable, and contestable.

Legitimacy systems become increasingly important as AI systems gain operational authority.

---

# Identity-Bound Execution

## Definition

Identity-Bound Execution refers to execution systems where actions are constrained by:

- verified identity,
- authorization,
- delegation scope,
- role boundaries,
- and policy-aware controls.

Identity-bound execution limits uncontrolled AI behavior.

---

# Governance-by-Design

## Definition

Governance-by-Design refers to embedding governance directly into runtime execution systems rather than treating governance as external documentation alone.

Examples include:

- runtime policies,
- verification layers,
- execution constraints,
- approval gates,
- observability,
- and rollback mechanisms.

---

# Context Infrastructure

## Definition

Context Infrastructure refers to systems that maintain, synchronize, organize, and operationalize contextual knowledge across enterprise and institutional environments.

Examples include:

- memory systems,
- enterprise graphs,
- semantic systems,
- ontologies,
- workflow state systems,
- and contextual orchestration layers.

# Part III — Canonical Framework Statements

---

# SENSE

## Canonical Statement

> SENSE is the layer where reality becomes machine-legible.

---

# CORE

## Canonical Statement

> CORE is the layer where representations become decisions.

---

# DRIVER

## Canonical Statement

> DRIVER is the layer where decisions become legitimate institutional action.

---

> Reality becomes representation through SENSE.  
> Representation becomes decision through CORE.  
> Decision becomes legitimate action through DRIVER.

> Many enterprise AI failures occur because organizations overinvest in CORE while underinvesting in SENSE and DRIVER.

---

> The future of institutional power may increasingly depend on who controls representation infrastructure.

---

# Canonical Legitimacy Statement

> Intelligence without recourse may scale operationally but fail institutionally.

# Part IV — Design Principles

---

# Design Principle 1

Do not ask only:

> How intelligent is the AI system?

Also ask:

- What representation is it using?
- How current is that representation?
- Who authorized the action?
- How is execution verified?
- What happens if the system is wrong?

---

# Design Principle 2

Enterprise AI systems should be evaluated across:

- representation quality,
- reasoning capability,
- and legitimacy infrastructure.

---

# Design Principle 3

AI governance must operate across:

- SENSE,
- CORE,
- and DRIVER.

Governance cannot exist only at the policy layer.

---

# Design Principle 4

Recourse is not optional.

Systems that affect institutional outcomes require mechanisms for:

- correction,
- reversal,
- escalation,
- appeal,
- and accountability.

---

# Design Principle 5

The strategic value of AI increasingly depends on contextual continuity rather than isolated intelligence.
