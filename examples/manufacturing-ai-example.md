# Manufacturing AI Example

## Intelligent Factories Require Representation, Reasoning, and Governance

Modern manufacturing increasingly uses AI for:
- predictive maintenance
- quality inspection
- robotics
- supply chain optimization
- production planning
- industrial automation

But manufacturing AI is not simply an automation problem.

It is also:
- a representation problem
- a contextual continuity problem
- a governed execution problem

---

# SENSE in Manufacturing

Factories generate massive streams of signals.

SENSE includes:
- machine telemetry
- sensor data
- equipment identity
- environmental conditions
- production states
- maintenance history
- supply chain context

Without SENSE:
- predictive systems fail
- machine context fragments
- operational continuity collapses

---

# CORE in Manufacturing

CORE includes:
- anomaly detection
- predictive maintenance
- production optimization
- robotic coordination
- planning systems
- adaptive scheduling

CORE answers:

> “What should the factory do?”

But stronger optimization alone is insufficient.

Poor representation may produce:
- incorrect maintenance decisions
- operational drift
- unstable automation
- cascading system failures

---

# DRIVER in Manufacturing

Industrial systems require governed execution.

DRIVER includes:
- safety verification
- operational boundaries
- escalation rules
- rollback systems
- human override
- auditability

Factories require bounded autonomy because unsafe actions can create:
- physical damage
- production instability
- human safety risks

---

# Why Manufacturing AI Requires SENSE–CORE–DRIVER

Manufacturing AI succeeds when:
- machines are represented correctly
- reasoning remains contextual
- execution remains governable

The framework argues that future factories may increasingly behave like:
- machine-coordinated cognitive systems
- continuously adaptive representation systems
- governed execution architectures
