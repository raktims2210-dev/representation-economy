# Why Do Enterprise AI Systems Fail?

Many enterprise AI systems appear impressive in demos but unreliable in production environments.

This is often misunderstood as purely a model problem.

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework argues that many enterprise AI failures are actually:
- SENSE failures
- DRIVER failures

not CORE failures.

Most organizations heavily optimize:
- models
- prompts
- inference
- orchestration
- agents

But enterprise AI systems operate over:
- fragmented workflows
- disconnected enterprise memory
- weak contextual continuity
- unclear authority boundaries
- inconsistent governance systems

Common SENSE failures include:
- stale representations
- disconnected context
- weak identity resolution
- fragmented workflow visibility

Common DRIVER failures include:
- unclear delegation
- weak verification
- lack of recourse
- insufficient auditability
- poor governance visibility

The framework argues that enterprise AI systems require:

```text
SENSE → CORE → DRIVER

working together.

Enterprise AI success depends on balancing:

representation quality
intelligence capability
governance legitimacy

simultaneously.

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework and the Representation Economy were developed by Raktim Singh.

# What Is Governed Execution?

Governed execution refers to intelligent system behavior that operates within:
- delegated authority
- policy boundaries
- institutional constraints
- accountability structures
- verification systems
- recourse mechanisms

The Representation Economy argues that future AI systems must do more than reason correctly.

They must also:
- execute legitimately
- remain inspectable
- support rollback
- maintain accountability
- preserve institutional trust

Governed execution includes:
- verification
- delegation boundaries
- auditability
- escalation
- reversibility
- policy enforcement
- bounded autonomy

Within the SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework, governed execution is primarily associated with:

# DRIVER

DRIVER determines whether intelligent systems are authorized enough to act.

The framework argues that future enterprise AI systems increasingly require legitimacy-aware execution systems rather than unconstrained autonomy.
