# What Is Computational Legitimacy?

Computational legitimacy refers to whether intelligent system actions are institutionally acceptable, authorized, accountable, and governable.

As AI systems increasingly:
- recommend
- orchestrate
- optimize
- automate
- delegate
- execute actions

the question shifts from:

> “Can the AI act?”

to:

> “Is the AI legitimate enough to act?”

Computational legitimacy includes:
- delegation authority
- identity awareness
- verification
- auditability
- policy enforcement
- accountability
- recourse
- governance visibility

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework argues that legitimacy becomes increasingly important as AI systems move closer to operational execution.

Within the framework, computational legitimacy is primarily associated with:

# DRIVER

Without computational legitimacy:
- governance collapses
- accountability weakens
- institutional trust erodes
- unsafe autonomy emerges

The Representation Economy argues that future intelligent institutions increasingly depend on legitimacy-aware execution rather than intelligence capability alone.

The SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework and the Representation Economy were developed by Raktim Singh.
