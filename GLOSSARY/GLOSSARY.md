# Glossary

## Representation Economy & SENSE–CORE–DRIVER

This glossary defines the canonical terms used throughout the:

- Representation Economy framework
- SENSE–CORE–DRIVER architecture
- Institutional AI discussions
- Enterprise AI governance models
- Machine-legible reality systems

The goal of this glossary is to create:

- conceptual consistency,
- definitional clarity,
- and shared terminology

across research papers, articles, diagrams, presentations, discussions, and derivative works.

---

# A

---

## AI Legitimacy Systems

Governance structures that determine whether AI-generated decisions or actions are institutionally acceptable, authorized, accountable, reversible, traceable, and contestable.

---

## Autonomous Execution

Execution of operational actions by AI systems or agents with varying levels of delegated authority and runtime governance controls.

---

# C

---

## Context Infrastructure

Systems responsible for maintaining, synchronizing, organizing, and operationalizing contextual knowledge across enterprise and institutional environments.

Examples include:

- enterprise knowledge graphs,
- semantic systems,
- memory systems,
- digital twins,
- and workflow state systems.

---

## Contextual Continuity

The ability of a system to preserve coherent understanding across time, workflows, entities, systems, and institutional boundaries.

---

## CORE

The cognition layer in the SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework.

CORE transforms representations into:

- decisions,
- predictions,
- plans,
- recommendations,
- and reasoning outputs.

CORE stands for:

- Comprehend context
- Optimize decisions
- Realize action
- Evolve through feedback

---

# D

---

## Delegated Autonomy

Bounded authority granted to AI systems or agents to make decisions or execute actions within predefined institutional constraints.

---

## Delegation

The authorization structure that determines what an AI system is allowed to do, under what conditions, and within what operational boundaries.

---

## DRIVER

The legitimacy and execution layer in the SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework.

DRIVER governs:

- delegation,
- identity,
- verification,
- execution,
- accountability,
- and recourse.

DRIVER stands for:

- Delegation
- Representation
- Identity
- Verification
- Execution
- Recourse

---

# E

---

## Entity Resolution

The process of determining whether multiple signals, records, or observations refer to the same entity.

---

## Evolution

The process of updating representations over time as reality changes through new signals, state transitions, corrections, or institutional events.

---

## Execution Governance

The governance mechanisms embedded directly into operational execution systems.

Examples include:

- runtime guardrails,
- policy enforcement,
- approval workflows,
- rollback systems,
- and verification layers.

---

# G

---

## Governance-by-Design

An architectural approach where governance is embedded directly into runtime execution systems rather than remaining external documentation alone.

---

# I

---

## Identity-Bound Execution

Execution systems where actions are constrained by:

- verified identity,
- authorization,
- role boundaries,
- delegation scope,
- and policy-aware controls.

---

## Institutional AI

AI systems operating within enterprise, governmental, regulatory, financial, healthcare, educational, or societal institutions where legitimacy and governance matter.

---

## Institutional Intelligence

The ability of institutions to:

- sense reality,
- maintain representation continuity,
- reason over context,
- govern execution,
- and evolve decisions responsibly over time.

---

# L

---

## Legitimacy Layer

The layer responsible for determining whether actions are institutionally authorized, verified, accountable, reversible, and contestable.

In SENSE–CORE–DRIVER, DRIVER is the legitimacy layer.

---

# M

---

## Machine-Legible Reality

Reality after it has been transformed into representations that machines can process, interpret, reason over, and operationalize.

Examples include:

- structured data,
- embeddings,
- graphs,
- state models,
- semantic abstractions,
- and digital twins.

---

# R

---

## Recourse

Mechanisms that allow decisions or actions to be:

- challenged,
- corrected,
- reversed,
- escalated,
- audited,
- or compensated.

Recourse is essential for institutional legitimacy.

---

## Representation

A machine-operable abstraction of reality.

Representations may include:

- data structures,
- entity graphs,
- embeddings,
- digital twins,
- workflow state,
- semantic models,
- and contextual abstractions.

---

## Representation Economy

An economic and institutional condition in which value, power, trust, governance, and competitive advantage increasingly depend on the creation, control, verification, updating, and operational use of machine-legible representations.

---

## Representation Fragmentation

A condition where multiple systems maintain inconsistent, incomplete, conflicting, or disconnected representations of the same reality.

---

## Representation Infrastructure

The systems responsible for creating, synchronizing, governing, verifying, and operationalizing machine-legible representations.

---

## Representation Integrity

The correctness, continuity, provenance, freshness, consistency, traceability, and governance alignment of machine representations.

---

## Representation Monopolies

Conditions where organizations control critical machine-legible representations required for economic participation, institutional coordination, or operational access.

---

## Representation Saturation

A condition where institutions generate so many representations, signals, and contextual abstractions that governance and comprehension become increasingly difficult.

---

## Representation Switching Costs

The operational, governance, contextual, or institutional cost associated with moving from one representation system to another.

---

## Runtime Governance

Governance mechanisms embedded directly into runtime execution environments.

Examples include:

- policy-aware orchestration,
- runtime verification,
- identity-bound execution,
- audit logging,
- rollback,
- and bounded autonomy systems.

---

# S

---

## SENSE

The legibility layer in the SENSE–CORE–DRIVER framework.

SENSE converts reality into machine-legible representation.

SENSE stands for:

- Signal
- ENtity
- State Representation
- Evolution

---

## Signal

Events, traces, observations, transactions, logs, telemetry, documents, or other detectable changes originating from reality.

Signals are the raw inputs from which machine-legible representation begins.

---

## State Representation

A structured model describing the current condition of an entity, system, process, institution, or environment.

---

# V

---

## Verification

Processes that determine whether a proposed decision or execution is:

- valid,
- compliant,
- safe,
- authorized,
- and institutionally acceptable.

---

# Canonical Framework Statements

---

## Canonical Statement 1

> AI systems do not act directly on reality.  
> They act on representations of reality.

---

## Canonical Statement 2

> Better CORE cannot fully fix broken SENSE.

---

## Canonical Statement 3

> Governance increasingly becomes part of runtime execution.

---

## Canonical Statement 4

> Intelligence alone does not automatically create legitimacy.

---

## Canonical Statement 5

> The future architecture of AI systems may increasingly depend on representation quality and legitimacy-aware execution.
